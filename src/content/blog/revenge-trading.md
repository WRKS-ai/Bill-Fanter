Every trader has a worst day. Mine cost me $74,000 — and the market only took part of it. The rest, I gave away myself, one angry trade at a time.

I tell the full story in the video above. This post is about the pattern behind it, because the pattern is universal. If you trade long enough, revenge trading *will* knock on your door. What matters is whether you have rules in place before it does.

## What revenge trading is

Revenge trading is what happens when you stop trading the market and start trading your emotions. You take a loss. It stings. And instead of stepping back, you jump straight into another trade — bigger, faster, sloppier — to "win it back."

The market doesn't know you're down. It doesn't owe you a recovery. But in that moment, you're not thinking in probabilities anymore. You're thinking like someone who got wronged and wants it back *now*.

## How one loss becomes a disaster

Here's the sequence, and if you've traded through it you'll recognize every step:

1. **The original loss.** A normal losing trade — the kind any system produces. On a calm day, you'd shrug it off.
2. **The refusal.** Instead of accepting it, you reframe it as unfair. Wrong entry, bad luck, "manipulation." The loss becomes personal.
3. **The oversized re-entry.** You double the size to get it back in one shot. The setup is worse, but the urgency is real.
4. **The spiral.** That trade loses too. Now you're down more, angrier, and the next position gets even bigger. Discipline is gone; the account is bleeding.

My $74K day followed that script almost exactly. The first loss was survivable. The next several — taken in anger, at the wrong size, with no setup — were not.

## The psychology underneath

Revenge trading isn't a strategy problem, it's a wiring problem. Losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good — that's loss aversion, and it's human. Your brain treats the drawdown as a threat and screams at you to fix it immediately.

That's why "just be disciplined" fails. In the moment, the emotional brain outshouts the rational one. The traders who survive aren't the ones who feel nothing — they're the ones who built rules *before* the moment, so the decision is already made.

## The rules that stop it

These are the guardrails I use and teach:

- **A daily loss limit.** Decide before the session how much you're allowed to lose. Hit it, and you're done for the day. No exceptions, no "one more."
- **Fixed position sizing.** Your size is set by your plan, never by your mood. If you feel the urge to double up to recover — that urge *is* the signal to walk away.
- **A cooling-off rule.** After any outsized loss, no new trades for a set period. The market will still be there tomorrow. Your capital might not be if you keep swinging.
- **Trade only written setups.** If you can't point to the setup in your plan, it isn't a trade — it's a bet. Revenge trades never survive this test.

This is also why I trade alongside a [community](/community) every morning. Isolation feeds the spiral; having other disciplined traders in the room breaks it.
