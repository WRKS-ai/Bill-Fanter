New traders almost always start with stocks. Buy a share, hope it goes up, sell it later. It feels safer because it's familiar. But after 35 years in banking and the markets, I trade options — and once you understand why, you may never look at plain stock trading the same way.

In the video above I walk through the full comparison on real charts. Here's the written version.

## The core difference

When you buy a stock, you're paying full price for every dollar of exposure. If you want a position in a $200 stock, 100 shares costs you $20,000 — and every dollar of that is at risk if the stock falls.

An option is a contract on that same stock. It gives you the right — not the obligation — to buy or sell at a set price before a set date. One contract controls 100 shares for a fraction of what the shares cost. That difference drives everything else.

## Why I choose options

### 1. Defined risk

When I buy an option, the most I can lose is what I paid for the contract. I know my worst case *before I enter the trade*. A stock can gap down through any stop-loss you set; a long option's maximum loss is fixed at the premium. For a rules-based trader, that certainty is worth a lot.

### 2. Less capital doing more work

Because a contract controls 100 shares, I can express the same idea with far less money tied up. That's not a license to over-leverage — position sizing still rules everything — but it means my capital isn't locked in one name while ten other setups pass me by.

### 3. More ways to be right

Stock traders profit only when the price goes their direction. Options let you build trades for a move up, a move down, or even a stock going nowhere. Selling premium in a sideways market is a strategy stock-only traders simply don't have.

### 4. Income strategies

Covered calls and cash-secured puts let you collect premium on positions you already hold or are willing to hold. It's one of the most practical ways to put a long-term portfolio to work.

## The honest trade-offs

Options aren't free money, and I teach them with the risks front and center:

- **Time decay.** Every option loses value as expiration approaches. Buy an option and go nowhere, and you can be "right eventually" but still lose. The clock is part of the trade.
- **Complexity.** Strikes, expirations, spreads, the Greeks — there's a real learning curve. Trading options without understanding them is how accounts get hurt.
- **Leverage cuts both ways.** The same math that amplifies gains amplifies percentage losses. Sizing and discipline matter more with options, not less.
