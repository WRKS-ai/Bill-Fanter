Ask a struggling trader what they're watching and you'll get one of two answers: "everything" or "whatever's moving today." Both are the same answer — no plan. Chasing whatever's already moving means arriving late to someone else's trade.

A watchlist is how you fix that. It's the difference between hunting for trades at 9:31 with your heart racing, and calmly executing a plan you wrote when the market was closed. ## Why you need one

The market has thousands of tickers. You can't watch them, and you don't need to. On any given week, only a handful are set up in a way that matches a repeatable edge. The watchlist's job is brutal filtering: from everything, down to the few names where you know *in advance* what you're looking for.

The real output isn't the list of tickers. It's the plan attached to each one — the level that matters, and what you'll do if price gets there.

## My weekly process

### Step 1: Start with the calendar

Before charts, I check what's scheduled: earnings, Fed meetings, inflation data, big product events. These are the catalysts that create movement — and the landmines that can blow up an otherwise good setup. A perfect chart with earnings on Wednesday is a different trade than the same chart with a quiet week ahead.

### Step 2: Scan for liquid, moving names

I narrow to stocks and ETFs that are liquid enough to trade well — tight options spreads, real volume — and that are actually in play: near key levels, showing momentum, or sitting on a catalyst. Illiquid names get cut no matter how pretty the chart is, because bad fills quietly tax every trade.

### Step 3: Mark the levels

For each surviving name, I mark the price levels that matter — support and resistance where buyers or sellers have shown up before, and the bounce levels I care about most. This is the heart of the list. A ticker without a level is just a symbol; a ticker *with* a level is a potential trade.

### Step 4: Write the "if-then"

For every name: *if* price does X at my level, *then* I do Y. If it never gets there, I do nothing — and doing nothing is a position. This is what makes the open calm instead of chaotic: the decisions were made on Sunday.

### Step 5: Keep it short

If my list grows past a dozen names, I cut it back. A 40-ticker watchlist is a screensaver, not a plan. Five names you know deeply beat fifty you're skimming.

## The mistakes that ruin watchlists

- **Building it during market hours.** You'll add whatever's green and exciting. Build it when the market is closed and your judgment is quiet.
- **Never removing anything.** A watchlist is a weekly rebuild, not a museum. If the setup resolved or died, the name comes off.
- **Lists without levels.** "Watching NVDA" is not a plan. "NVDA at 180, watching for a bounce" is.
- **Ignoring the calendar.** Getting blindsided by a scheduled event is an unforced error. The schedule is public.
