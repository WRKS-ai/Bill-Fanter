Turn on any financial channel during a selloff and you'll hear it: "the VIX is spiking." Traders call it the fear gauge. But most explanations stop at the nickname, and that's a problem — because if you trade options, the VIX isn't trivia. It's priced into every contract you buy or sell.

In the video above I break down what the VIX actually measures and when volatility gets dangerous. Here's the foundation.

## What the VIX actually measures

The VIX (the Cboe Volatility Index) measures how much movement the options market *expects* from the S&P 500 over the next 30 days. It's calculated from the prices traders are actually paying for S&P 500 options right now.

That's the key insight: the VIX isn't an opinion poll. It's real money making real bets. When traders expect turbulence, they pay more for protection, and the VIX rises. When they expect calm, protection gets cheap, and the VIX falls.

## How to read the levels

There's no magic number, but after decades of watching it, here's the practical map I use:

- **Below ~15 — calm.** Markets are quiet, options are cheap, moves are small. Complacency can build here.
- **15–20 — normal.** Ordinary two-sided trading. Most of the market's life is spent in this zone.
- **20–30 — elevated.** Something has traders paying up for protection: an election, a Fed meeting, earnings season, a geopolitical scare.
- **Above 30 — fear.** Real stress. Big daily swings in both directions. This is where fortunes are made and destroyed — usually destroyed by traders who didn't adjust.

## Why options traders can't ignore it

The VIX describes the same force that sets the price of every option you trade: implied volatility.

**When the VIX is high, options are expensive.** Buying calls or puts costs more, so you need a bigger move just to break even. Strategies that *sell* premium start to make more sense — you're being paid more to take the other side.

**When the VIX is low, options are cheap.** Buying premium costs less, but the market is also telling you it expects small moves. The trade that works in a VIX-30 world can be exactly wrong in a VIX-13 world.

Same chart, same stock, same setup — the right options strategy can be completely different depending on the volatility you're paying for. That's why volatility is one of the first things I check before any trade, and it's a core module in [the masterclass](/masterclass).

## When volatility gets dangerous

High VIX environments create huge ranges. A stock that normally moves 1% a day starts moving 4%. That cuts both ways: winners get bigger, but losers get bigger faster, and stop-losses turn unreliable when prices gap.

The mistake I see most is traders keeping their normal position size in an abnormal market. If daily ranges triple, your risk per trade just tripled too — unless you cut your size. In dangerous volatility, professional traders trade *smaller*, not bigger.
